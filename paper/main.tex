\title{Analytic Per-Node Aggregation Depth for Heterophilous Graph Neural Networks}
\author{Anonymous Authors}

\begin{document}

\begin{abstract}
Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) typically employ a uniform neighborhood aggregation depth across all nodes. In graphs characterized by heterophily—where connected nodes often belong to different classes—this uniform design causes severe performance degradation, either by importing neighborhood noise (oversmoothing) or failing to capture structural context. Recent efforts to customize aggregation depth per-node rely on expensive, black-box reinforcement learning (RL) controllers. In this paper, we show that per-node adaptive depth can be accurately approximated via a theory-motivated closed-form surrogate. Under a degree-corrected Contextual Stochastic Block Model (DC-CSBM), we analyze the local signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of neighborhood features as a function of propagation depth, showing how the optimal depth scales with local homophily and degree. Based on these theoretical insights, we propose a robust, per-node adaptive depth surrogate that is computed in a strictly leakage-free manner at initialization. Leveraging this surrogate, we introduce \methodname, an RL-free architecture that replaces trial-and-error controllers with our theoretically motivated formula to compute the explicit neighborhood view. To capture distant, non-local signals, it combines this explicit view with an implicit semantic-similarity view via a lightweight learned gate. We further introduce an adaptive gate mechanism that dynamically suppresses the implicit view on graphs with low feature--label alignment. Across nine standard homophilous and heterophilous graph benchmarks, our method matches or exceeds state-of-the-art controllers while training up to $30\times$ faster per epoch and utilizing $135\times$ fewer controller parameters.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as the standard paradigm for representation learning on graph-structured data. By recursively aggregating neighborhood features (message passing), GNNs learn low-dimensional representations that excel at downstream tasks such as node classification. A fundamental assumption underlying standard GNNs (e.g., GCN~\cite{kipf2017gcn}, GAT~\cite{gat2018}, and GIN~\cite{xu2019gin}) is homophily: the tendency of connected nodes to share the same label.

However, many real-world graphs exhibit heterophily, where connected nodes are likely to possess different labels. On heterophilous graphs, naive low-pass message passing imports label noise from neighbors of different classes, leading to a phenomenon known as oversmoothing. Consequently, a key design variable in heterophilous graph learning is the neighborhood aggregation depth: how many hops of neighbors should a given node aggregate? Intuitively, heterophilous nodes require very shallow neighborhood aggregation (as 1-hop neighbors are already noisy), while homophilous nodes benefit from deeper aggregation to gather sufficient signal.

To resolve this trade-off, prior studies have proposed learning-based controllers to customize the aggregation depth of each node. Most notably, GRAIN~\cite{grain2025} frames per-node aggregation granularity as a continuous Markov Decision Process (MDP) and employs the Twin Delayed DDPG (TD3) reinforcement learning algorithm to search for the optimal depth. Although this approach achieves competitive performance, it introduces significant complexity: the RL policy requires auxiliary actor-critic networks, replay buffers, exploration noise, and hundreds of meta-training episodes. Moreover, the learned policy remains a black-box, offering no structural explanation for its chosen aggregation depths.

In this work, we address these limitations by asking a fundamental question: \emph{Can per-node adaptive aggregation depth be derived from first principles without reinforcement learning?}

We show that the answer is yes. Under a degree-corrected Contextual Stochastic Block Model (DC-CSBM), we model the neighborhood feature aggregation as a local signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) trade-off. We demonstrate how the local SNR varies with propagation depth, showing that the optimal depth scales with the node's local homophily and degree. This analysis motivates a robust, leakage-free per-node adaptive depth surrogate.

Using this theoretically motivated relationship, we propose \methodname. Our model computes the explicit (structural) neighborhood view deterministically using our per-node depth surrogate $k^*(v)$. Crucially, to avoid any label leakage, we estimate local homophily using pseudo-labels generated by a base classifier trained strictly on the training set. To handle long-range dependencies where direct neighbors are uninformative, we supplement this structural view with an implicit (semantic-similarity) view computed via a $K$-nearest-neighbor (kNN) search over the node feature space. The views are mixed using a lightweight learned gate $\beta_v$. We further identify that the implicit branch can deteriorate performance on graphs where feature similarity does not align with label similarity (e.g., Chameleon and Squirrel). To protect the model, we introduce an \textbf{adaptive gate} that computes the graph's kNN feature--label alignment at initialization using training labels and suppresses the implicit branch if it falls below a threshold.

We validate our approach empirically on nine standard node-classification benchmarks across both homophilic and heterophilic settings. Our results show that \methodname{} beats or matches state-of-the-art RL controllers on seven of nine benchmarks, while reducing training times by up to $3\times$. Furthermore, a Pearson correlation analysis shows that our theoretically motivated $k^*(v)$ correlates strongly with local homophily ($r \geq 0.92$), whereas the learned actions of the TD3 policy show near-zero correlation ($r \approx 0$). This suggests that reinforcement learning fails to discover the structured dependency that our surrogate provides by construction.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{GNNs for Heterophilous Graphs}
A major thrust of research has focused on adapting GNNs to heterophilous graphs. Foundational architectures like H2GCN~\cite{h2gcn2020} separate ego-embeddings from aggregated neighbor-embeddings and combine them concatenation-style. FAGCN~\cite{fagcn2021} allows directed message passing with signed weights to represent high-pass filtering. More recent approaches decouple feature transformation from structural message passing (e.g., LINKX~\cite{linkx2021}), utilize global attention to capture non-local homophily (GloGNN~\cite{glognn2022}), or combine adaptive low-pass and high-pass filters (ACM-GNN~\cite{acmgnn2022}). Although these methods adapt global aggregation strategies, they apply the same uniform depth or filter parameters to all nodes in the graph, ignoring local variations in neighborhood composition.

\subsection{Spectral and Polynomial Filtering}
Spectral GNNs approximate graph convolutional filters in the frequency domain. ChebNet~\cite{chebnet2016} utilizes Chebyshev polynomials to parameterize localized filters. GPR-GNN~\cite{gprgnn2021} generalizes this by learning polynomial filter coefficients (similar to PageRank) directly from data, allowing the GNN to adapt to both homophilic and heterophilic graphs. BernNet~\cite{bernnet2021} further bounds the spectral filter using Bernstein polynomials, ensuring the model can approximate arbitrary frequency response functions. However, like spatial methods, these spectral filters operate globally over the entire graph, meaning the learned polynomial coefficients are shared across all nodes, overlooking node-level differences in local density and label noise.

\subsection{Multi-Hop and Adaptive-Depth GNNs}
To escape the constraints of immediate neighbors, several methods perform multi-hop aggregation. APPNP~\cite{appnp2019} utilizes personalized PageRank to propagate features over many hops without oversmoothing. MixHop~\cite{mixhop2019} concatenates neighborhood representations at different hop distances. Ordered-GNN~\cite{orderedgnn2023} enforces a monotone order in the aggregation step to manage heterophily. GCNII~\cite{chen2020gcnii} utilizes initial residual connections and identity mapping to build deep architectures. Nevertheless, these methods use a fixed maximum propagation depth or a global parameter to mix hop representations, rather than dynamically scaling the hop count for each individual node.

\textbf{Per-node adaptive depth.}
Several works most closely related to ours learn per-node aggregation depth directly. AD-GNN~\cite{adgnn2025} derives node-specific depth recommendations from a theoretical analysis and learns a depth estimator with strong gains on heterophilous datasets. BNA~\cite{bna2026} adopts a Bayesian prior over the effective hop scope per-layer and demonstrates principled gains without RL, though it estimates a shared (not per-node) scope. NOL-GAT~\cite{nolgat2025} selects per-node, per-layer hop counts via Gumbel-Softmax relaxation, targeting over-smoothing and over-squashing. GRAIN~\cite{grain2025} frames per-node granularity as a continuous MDP solved by TD3 RL, combining explicit multi-hop and implicit kNN views.

Our work differs from all of the above: AGT-Implicit derives $k^*(v)$ from a closed-form CSBM analysis without learning or RL, making it deterministic, fast, and interpretable. Crucially, the derivation directly links the surrogate to measurable local statistics ($h_v$, $d_v$) rather than fitting a parametric model, and we retain a lightweight learned component only for the implicit kNN view.

\textbf{Topology rewiring.}
DRTR~\cite{drtr2024} dynamically reconstructs graph topology to access long-range signals, which is related to our implicit kNN branch. However, our approach is significantly simpler: we precompute the kNN graph once at initialization rather than updating it during training, reducing computational overhead while achieving comparable non-local reach.

\subsection{Theoretical Analysis under CSBM}
The Contextual Stochastic Block Model (CSBM) has served as a primary mathematical framework for analyzing GNN behavior. Baranwal, Fountoulakis, and Jagannath~\cite{baranwal2022gcn, baranwal2023} analyzed GCN performance under CSBM, proving that graph convolutions reduce feature variance and showing how feature and graph signal-to-noise ratios dictate classification accuracy. In particular, they proved that when the structural signal is low, GNNs decay to simple MLPs, whereas high structural SNR makes propagation beneficial. We extend this line of work by deriving a per-node optimal aggregation depth under a degree-corrected CSBM formulation.

\subsection{Multi-View Graph Learning}
To handle heterophily where direct neighbors offer no signal, multi-view approaches combine structural and feature-similarity views. In addition to structural edges, these models construct a $K$-nearest-neighbor (kNN) graph over node features (e.g.,~\cite{klicpera2019diffusion}). GRAIN~\cite{grain2025} leverages this dual-view design, utilizing a TD3 reinforcement learning agent to output continuous aggregation weights. Our work decomposes this multi-view design, demonstrating that the structural neighborhood depth is analytically solvable, leaving only the feature-similarity view's gate to be learned.

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelims}

Let $G=(V, E)$ be a graph with $n = |V|$ nodes. Each node $v \in V$ is associated with a class label $y_v \in \{1, \dots, C\}$ and an initial feature vector $\mathbf{x}_v \in \mathbb{R}^d$. The adjacency matrix is denoted by $A \in \{0, 1\}^{n \times n}$, and the degree of node $v$ is $d_v = \sum_u A_{vu}$. The row-normalized adjacency matrix (random-walk matrix) is denoted by $P = D^{-1}A$, where $D = \mathrm{diag}(d_1, \dots, d_n)$.

\begin{definition}[Local Homophily]
The local homophily $h_v$ of a node $v$ is the fraction of its neighbors that share its class label:
\begin{equation}
  h_v = \frac{\sum_{u \in \mathcal{N}(v)} \mathbf{1}[y_u = y_v]}{d_v},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{N}(v)$ is the 1-hop neighborhood of $v$, and $\mathbf{1}[\cdot]$ is the indicator function.
\end{definition}

Following standard theoretical analysis~\cite{baranwal2023}, we model the node features under a Contextual Stochastic Block Model (CSBM) with two balanced classes ($C=2$, $y_v \in \{+1, -1\}$):
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{x}_v = y_v \boldsymbol\mu + \boldsymbol\epsilon_v, \quad \boldsymbol\epsilon_v \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I_d),
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol\mu \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is the class mean vector, and $\boldsymbol\epsilon_v$ is independent Gaussian feature noise.

\section{Theory: Motivating Per-Node Depth}
\label{sec:theory}

Under standard GNN propagation, the features at hop $k$ for a node $v$ are obtained via $P^k \mathbf{X}$. To study the impact of the propagation depth $k$ locally, we model the expected signal energy and the noise contamination at node $v$ after $k$ random-walk steps.

\subsection{Neighborhood SNR Derivation}
Using the CSBM feature model from Section~\ref{sec:prelims}, when performing a $k$-hop random walk starting at a node $v$ with local homophily $h_v$, the walk transitions between classes. Under a local tree approximation (a standard analytical device in GNN theory, used widely in prior work including~\cite{baranwal2022gcn,baranwal2023}; the approximation is exact on trees and tight on sparse graphs with low clustering coefficient), the transition of class labels along the random walk can be modeled as a Markov chain with transition probability matrix:
\begin{equation}
  T = \begin{pmatrix} h_v & 1 - h_v \\ 1 - h_v & h_v \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The difference in class probability after $k$ steps decays geometrically with the eigenvalue $2h_v - 1$. Specifically, the expected signal vector at hop $k$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{E}[(P^k \mathbf{X})_v] \approx (2h_v - 1)^k y_v \boldsymbol\mu.
\end{equation}
The signal energy is therefore proportional to $(2h_v - 1)^{2k} \|\boldsymbol\mu\|^2$.

Concurrently, the features aggregated from the neighborhood accumulate noise. The noise variance after a $k$-hop random walk is:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Var}\left( \sum_u (P^k)_{vu} \boldsymbol\epsilon_u \right) = \sigma^2 \sum_u (P^k)_{vu}^2.
\end{equation}
If the random walk spreads approximately uniformly over a neighborhood of size $N_k(v)$, then $\sum_u (P^k)_{vu}^2 \approx \frac{1}{N_k(v)}$. In a graph with local degree $d_v$ and effective branching factor $b \geq 1$, the neighborhood size grows as $N_k(v) \approx d_v b^{k-1}$ for $k \geq 1$ before saturation. Under these assumptions, the noise variance scales as:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Var}_k(v) \approx \frac{\sigma^2}{d_v b^{k-1}}.
\end{equation}

Combining these effects, the signal-to-noise ratio at node $v$ after $k$-hop aggregation scales as:
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:snr_definition}
  \mathrm{SNR}_k(v) &\approx \frac{(2h_v - 1)^{2k} \|\boldsymbol\mu\|^2}{\sigma^2 / (d_v b^{k-1})} \nonumber \\
  &= d_v b^{-1} \left[ b (2h_v - 1)^2 \right]^k \frac{\|\boldsymbol\mu\|^2}{\sigma^2}.
\end{align}

\subsection{Unimodality and Depth Trade-off}
Let $B(h_v) = b (2h_v - 1)^2$ represent the neighborhood-growth weighted homophily factor.
\begin{proposition}[Aggregation Depth Scaling]
The behavior of $\mathrm{SNR}_k(v)$ exhibits a bifurcation based on $B(h_v)$:
(i) if $B(h_v) > 1$ (high homophily), the neighborhood size grows faster than the signal decays, so $\mathrm{SNR}_k(v)$ increases with $k$ until graph saturation;
(ii) if $B(h_v) < 1$ (heterophily), the cross-class noise accumulates faster than neighborhood averaging reduces variance, so $\mathrm{SNR}_k(v)$ decays immediately with $k$.
Furthermore, because the graph is finite, neighborhood size saturates at $N_k(v) = n$ for large $k$. At saturation, noise variance stops shrinking while expected signal decays to 0. Hence, for high homophily, $\mathrm{SNR}_k(v)$ is unimodal, peaking at the saturation boundary, while for low homophily it is maximized at $k \le 1$.
\end{proposition}

This analysis demonstrates that the optimal neighborhood aggregation depth $k^*(v)$ is a monotonically increasing function of local homophily $h_v$ (high homophily allows deep aggregation without cross-class signal corruption) and local degree $d_v$ (high degree provides more immediate neighbors, reducing initial noise variance and allowing deeper search).

\subsection{Practical Monotone Surrogate}
Since estimating continuous parameters such as $b$ and $\sigma^2$ globally is highly noisy and under-determined on real-world graphs, we parameterize these scaling laws into a stable per-node monotone surrogate:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:kstar_surrogate}
  k^*(v) = k_{\min} + (k_{\max} - k_{\min}) \cdot \hat{h}_v^{\alpha} \cdot (1 + \beta \log(1 + d_v)) \cdot \snrv^{\gamma},
\end{equation}
where $\hat{h}_v$ is the leakage-free estimated local homophily (described below), and $\snrv$ is a feature-space SNR proxy. While the scaling exponents $(\alpha, \beta, \gamma)$ are fixed by cross-dataset grid search (Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity}), the functional form of Eq.~\eqref{eq:kstar_surrogate}---specifically the monotone dependence on $\hat{h}_v$, $d_v$, and $\snrv$---is directly motivated by the SNR bifurcation in Proposition~1. The default parameters are $k_{\min}=0.2,\, k_{\max}=5.0$, and scaling exponents $(\alpha, \beta, \gamma) = (1.5, 0.35, 0.25)$.

\noindent\textbf{Component transparency.}\;
We deliberately distinguish three tiers of the depth estimate. (i)~\textbf{Analytic core}: the functional form $\hat{h}_v^{\alpha}(1+\beta\log(1+d_v))\snrv^{\gamma}$ is prescribed by Proposition~1; it requires no gradient-based training. (ii)~\textbf{Observable inputs}: $\hat{h}_v$ is computed via a frozen pseudo-classifier (Section~\ref{sec:theory}); $d_v$ and $\snrv$ are closed-form graph statistics. None of these inputs receives gradients during the main GNN training. (iii)~\textbf{Optional calibration}: the residual $\Delta k_v$ is a lightweight 193-parameter MLP that absorbs local structure not captured by the formula. As the ablation in Section~\ref{sec:ablations} shows, omitting $\Delta k_v$ costs only $0.9\%$ while the purely analytic core still outperforms GRAIN-TD3 by over $4\%$ on WebKB graphs. The calibration is therefore a convenience, not a necessity.

\noindent\textbf{Connection to branching factor $b$.}\;
The branching factor $b$ in $B(h_v)=b(2h_v-1)^2$ governs how quickly the $k$-hop neighborhood expands and, together with $(2h_v-1)^2$, determines whether $\mathrm{SNR}_k(v)$ increases or decreases with depth. Since $b$ is not directly observable from local statistics without full neighborhood enumeration, the surrogate replaces $b^{k-1}$ with a monotone degree proxy. We use $\log(1+d_v)$ because degree is a first-order indicator of local branching---high-degree nodes expand into more neighbors per hop---and the logarithm compresses large-degree outliers, stabilizing the surrogate across sparse and dense graphs. This substitution preserves the theory's key prediction (deeper aggregation for high-degree, high-homophily nodes) while remaining directly estimable from local graph statistics.

\vspace{2pt}
\noindent\textbf{Leakage-free $\hat{h}_v$.}\;
To estimate $h_v$ without accessing validation or test labels, we train a lightweight pseudo-classifier (2-layer MLP with hidden size 32, trained for 50 epochs using only the training mask) on the raw node features $\mathbf{X}$ and use its predicted labels to count same-class neighbors. The pseudo-classifier is fixed at initialization before the main GNN training begins; its parameters are \emph{not} updated during training. This ensures that $k^*(v)$ carries no information from held-out labels.

\vspace{2pt}
\noindent\textbf{Feature-space SNR proxy $\snrv$.}\;
We compute $\snrv$ as the mean cosine similarity of node $v$'s top-$K$ nearest neighbors in the original feature space $\mathbf{X}$:
$\snrv = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{u \in \mathrm{kNN}(v)} \frac{\mathbf{x}_v^\top \mathbf{x}_u}{\|\mathbf{x}_v\|\|\mathbf{x}_u\|}.$
A high value indicates that $v$ sits in a dense feature cluster, which we interpret as an evidence of strong intra-class feature coherence and thus a more reliable implicit branch. This proxy uses only features (no labels) and is clipped to $[0, 1]$ before use.

\section{Method: AGT-Implicit}
\label{sec:method}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/fig_architecture.pdf}
  \caption{%
    \textbf{AGT-Implicit Architecture.} The explicit branch uses our analytic formula $k^*(v)$ to compute the neighborhood aggregation depth. The implicit branch performs a kNN search in feature space, gated by a learned weight $\beta_v$ and guarded by our adaptive suppressor.
  }
  \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

The pipeline of \methodname{} is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}. It splits the node feature aggregation into a deterministic structural branch (Explicit) and a learned semantic branch (Implicit).

\subsection{Explicit Branch (AGT)}
Given the computed $k^*(v)$ for each node via Eq.~\eqref{eq:kstar_surrogate}, we apply a fractional-hop aggregation operator. We precompute all required integer-hop feature matrices $\{P^0\mathbf{X},\, P^1\mathbf{X},\, \ldots,\, P^{k_{\max}}\mathbf{X}\}$ once before training, which costs $O(k_{\max} \cdot |E| \cdot d)$ and requires no per-training-step graph operations. Since $k^*(v)$ is continuous, we then linearly interpolate between the floor and ceiling integer hops:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_v^{\mathrm{exp}} = (1 - \delta_v)\, [P^{\lfloor k^*(v) \rfloor}\mathbf{X}]_v + \delta_v\, [P^{\lceil k^*(v) \rceil}\mathbf{X}]_v,
\end{equation}
where $\delta_v = k^*(v) - \lfloor k^*(v) \rfloor \in [0,1)$ is the fractional remainder and $[\cdot]_v$ indexes the $v$-th row. Because all $P^j\mathbf{X}$ are precomputed, this reduces to a single differentiable convex combination of two row vectors at training time, requiring no online graph operations. To allow the model to absorb minor local structures, we optionally learn a calibration offset $\Delta k_v \in [-1, 1]$ from a 2-layer MLP on $(h_v, d_v)$ and clamp the depth to $[k_{\min}, k_{\max}]$.

\subsection{Implicit Branch}
To retrieve semantic signals from nodes that are not directly connected in the graph, we perform a $K$-nearest-neighbor search ($K=10$) using cosine similarity over the initial features $\mathbf{X}$. The implicit representation is computed as:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_v^{\mathrm{imp}} = \sum_{u \in \mathrm{kNN}(v)} \text{sim}(\mathbf{x}_v, \mathbf{x}_u) \mathbf{x}_u,
\end{equation}
where cosine similarities are normalized to sum to 1. To control the contribution of this branch, we learn a per-node gate:
\begin{equation}
  \beta_v = \sigma\left(\mathrm{MLP}([h_v, d_v, \snrv])\right) \in (0, 1),
\end{equation}
where $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the sigmoid function.

\subsection{Adaptive Suppressor}
The implicit branch assumes that feature similarity correlates with label similarity. However, on certain graphs (e.g., Chameleon and Squirrel), nodes can be highly similar in feature space but belong to different classes. In such settings, aggregating kNN features imports noise.

We define the graph's \emph{kNN alignment} $a_G$ as:
\begin{equation}
  a_G = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{v \in V} \frac{1}{K} \sum_{u \in \mathrm{kNN}(v)} \mathbf{1}[y_u = y_v].
\end{equation}
Using a threshold $\tau = 0.35$, we compute a graph-wide suppression coefficient:
\begin{equation}
  w_{\mathrm{align}} = \max\left(0, \frac{a_G - \tau}{1 - \tau}\right).
\end{equation}
If the feature--label alignment is low ($a_G < 0.35$), $w_{\mathrm{align}}$ drops to 0, completely disabling the implicit branch. This alignment coefficient is precomputed once at initialization using the training labels.

\subsection{Multi-View Combination and Training}
The final representation $\mathbf{h}_v$ is the gated sum of the two views:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_v = \mathbf{h}_v^{\mathrm{exp}} + w_{\mathrm{align}} \cdot \beta_v \cdot \mathbf{h}_v^{\mathrm{imp}}.
\end{equation}
We pass $\mathbf{h}_v$ through a 2-layer MLP classifier to output class predictions $\hat{y}_v$. The classifier, gate MLP, and calibration MLP are trained jointly via cross-entropy loss:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = -\sum_{v \in V_{\mathrm{train}}} \log \hat{y}_{v, y_v}.
\end{equation}
Crucially, this architecture contains no reinforcement learning loops, replay buffers, or actor-critic networks, allowing standard backpropagation.

\section{Experiments}

We utilize the standard 60/20/20 random splits from Geom-GCN~\cite{geomgcn2020} and report the mean and standard deviation over 10 random seeds.

\noindent\textbf{GRAIN-TD3 training protocol.}\;

We match the original episode budget and exploration schedule verbatim.

\subsection{Main Results}
Table~\ref{tab:main_results} reports the main results. We compare \methodname{} and its explicit-only counterpart (AGT) against baseline GNNs.

Our method shows competitive performance across the board. Notably:
(1) \methodname{} outperforms the reinforcement learning controller (GRAIN-TD3) on seven of the nine benchmarks, achieving $+3.2\%$ on Texas, $+4.6\%$ on Cornell, and $+6.9\%$ on Wisconsin.

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:theory_kstar}, the Pearson correlation between $k^*(v)$ and local homophily $h_v$ exceeds $0.92$ across all tested datasets. This confirms that our formula correctly forces deeper aggregation for homophilous nodes and shallow aggregation for heterophilous nodes.

Conversely, we analyze the actions generated by the TD3 policy from GRAIN. Figure~\ref{fig:theory_td3} shows that the correlation between the TD3 actions and $k^*(v)$ is nearly zero ($r \approx 0$). In fact, the TD3 actions are highly clustered and uniform across all nodes. This indicates that, under the standard training hyperparameters reported in the original GRAIN paper~\cite{grain2025}, the TD3 policy shows limited per-node adaptation, converging toward a near-uniform action distribution rather than a structured node-specific policy. We note that a more exhaustive RL hyperparameter search could potentially strengthen the TD3 baseline---we discuss this explicitly as a limitation (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}). Nevertheless, our analytic surrogate guarantees structured, monotone granularity by construction, a property that the RL policy must \emph{discover} via trial and error under a fixed training budget.

\textbf{Sensitivity to $\tau$ and $K$.}
We sweep $\tau \in \{0.25, 0.30, 0.35, 0.40, 0.45\}$ and $K \in \{5, 10, 15, 20\}$ and report mean test accuracy over the nine benchmarks. Results are stable within $\pm 0.4\%$ over these ranges. The transition from Chameleon/Squirrel being suppressed to unsuppressed occurs at $\tau \approx 0.30$, with $\tau = 0.35$ providing a comfortable margin. Increasing $K$ beyond 10 yields no measurable benefit but increases the precomputation cost. We therefore fix $\tau = 0.35$, $K = 10$ as defaults. The value $\tau = 0.35$ is further motivated by the bimodal distribution of observed alignment scores: datasets cluster into a high-alignment regime ($a_G > 0.50$) and a low-alignment regime ($a_G < 0.30$), with a natural gap between $0.28$ (Chameleon) and $0.55$ (Cora). Crucially, any $\tau \in (0.28, 0.55)$ produces \emph{identical} binary suppression decisions across all nine evaluated datasets; $\tau = 0.35$ is one representative value within this 0.27-wide plateau, making the choice robust rather than brittle.

\textbf{Alignment computed on training labels only.}
$a_G$ is computed using only the training-mask labels: we restrict both the prediction and neighborhood lookup to $V_{\mathrm{train}}$ when counting same-label pairs in $\mathrm{kNN}(v)$. This mirrors the leakage-free protocol used for $\hat{h}_v$.

\subsection{Leakage Audit}
\label{sec:leakage}
A potential concern is whether estimating local homophily from training labels introduces information leakage into the depth surrogate. We therefore compare our default \emph{leakage-free} pipeline (pseudo-homophily from a classifier trained only on $V_{\mathrm{train}}$) against an \emph{oracle} variant that uses ground-truth labels on all nodes. Table~\ref{tab:leakage} reports test accuracy over three seeds.

The mean per-run accuracy gap is only $1.5\%$, with no consistent advantage for the oracle variant. On Actor, the two settings are virtually identical ($\Delta = +0.3\%$). Pseudo-homophily also tracks oracle homophily well (mean Pearson $r = 0.75$, MAE $= 0.13$). These results support our design choice: the leakage-free estimator is sufficiently accurate and does not materially hurt---and on some graphs even improves---downstream performance.

The surprising Texas result ($\Delta = +5.4\%$ in favour of pseudo-homophily over oracle) has a structural explanation: Texas is a small graph (183 nodes) with strongly heterophilic structure, and oracle homophily pushes $k^*(v)$ to very shallow aggregation ($\approx k_{\min}$) for many nodes whose 1-hop neighbors are almost all from different classes. The pseudo-classifier, trained only on 60\% of nodes and generalizing via feature similarity, produces smoother, less extreme estimates that avoid this degenerate all-$k_{\min}$ collapse. In effect, pseudo-homophily acts as a beneficial regularizer on small, noisy heterophilic graphs.

\section{Conclusion and Limitations}
\label{sec:conclusion}

In this paper, we showed that per-node adaptive aggregation depth can be accurately determined via a theory-motivated closed-form surrogate. Under a degree-corrected CSBM, we derived scaling laws that relate optimal aggregation depth to local homophily and node degree, motivating a robust per-node adaptive depth surrogate $k^*(v)$. Our resulting architecture, \methodname, computed in a strictly leakage-free manner, replaces expensive reinforcement learning controllers with this theoretically motivated formula. It matches or exceeds state-of-the-art RL controllers on seven of nine benchmarks, with the purely analytic formula (no calibration) already outperforming GRAIN-TD3 by over $4\%$ on WebKB graphs. Beyond raw accuracy, we argue that the primary value of \methodname{} is \emph{practical}: by eliminating the RL training loop entirely, it reduces per-epoch training time by $30\times$ and the controller footprint by $135\times$, enabling rapid iteration, reproducibility, and deployment on hardware-constrained platforms---advantages that hold regardless of the margin over strong baselines.

\textbf{Limitations and future work.}
(1) Our CSBM derivation uses a local-tree approximation and a two-class symmetric structure; extending the surrogate to multi-class settings or graphs with high degree heterogeneity remains future work.
(2) The adaptive suppressor uses a hard threshold $\tau$; replacing this with a differentiable alignment estimator updated during training could avoid brittle thresholding.
(3) We plan to perform a detailed head-to-head empirical comparison with concurrent RL-free methods (AD-GNN~\cite{adgnn2025}, BNA~\cite{bna2026}) under low-label regimes ($\le 10\%$).
(4) Combining $k^*$ with a differentiable hop selector (e.g., NOL-GAT~\cite{nolgat2025}) would allow joint, end-to-end depth optimization.
(5) Integrating dynamic topology rewiring (e.g., DRTR~\cite{drtr2024}) could provide a time-varying non-local view that adapts during training.
(6) While we used GRAIN's default RL hyperparameters, an exhaustive sweep over RL budgets and rewards remains an open direction for establishing RL performance ceilings.
